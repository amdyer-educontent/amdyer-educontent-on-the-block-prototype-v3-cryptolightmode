This Week in Crypto

Three Deadlines Converging. Here’s What to Watch.

Three significant catalysts are coming at the end of April, each with the potential to influence market sentiment.

First, the two-week U.S.–Iran ceasefire announced April 7 is scheduled to expire April 22. Talks in Islamabad concluded April 12 without an agreement, and as of this writing no deal has yet been struck. Any diplomatic development between now and the expiry date could affect oil prices, inflation expectations, and broader risk appetite — all factors that have weighed on crypto markets since the conflict began in late February. [2]

Second, the Senate Banking Committee is expected to move into a markup session for the CLARITY Act in the next week or two, according to Senator Bill Hagerty. A markup session is the formal process by which a congressional committee reviews, amends, and votes on a bill before sending it to the full chamber for consideration. The CLARITY Act is designed to establish a formal U.S. regulatory framework for digital assets, dividing oversight between the SEC and CFTC and providing statutory classification for crypto assets. [3]

“If we don’t get the Clarity Act passed by May, digital asset legislation will not pass for the foreseeable future.”

Sen. Bernie Moreno — DC Blockchain Summit [4]

Third, the Fed meets next week, on April 28 and 29 — a deadline of sorts for central bankers to offer their analysis of how an increasingly complicated geopolitical situation and higher oil prices may be influencing inflation.

What it means for you: These are genuinely uncertain situations, and how they play out could move markets in multiple directions. For investors with a long time horizon, understanding these factors as context for any volatility you observe this week — rather than as signals to act on — may be a useful frame. [A]

Feature

Crypto-Backed Mortgages: A New Path to Homeownership

Andrei Jikh, crypto educator & SoFi partner

Most homebuyers don’t have cash sitting around for a down payment — their money is tied up in stocks, retirement accounts, or crypto. Selling those assets to raise cash typically triggers a significant tax bill. A new mortgage product from lender Better Home & Finance and Coinbase aims to solve this problem for crypto holders by letting them pledge Bitcoin or USDC as collateral instead of a cash down payment. [P1]

Why It Matters

With housing affordability at its worst in decades and roughly one in seven American adults owning crypto, expanding how buyers can qualify for a home loan could draw more people into the market.

“What was once a highly speculative experiment could now be mainstream enough to finance one of the hallmarks of the American dream.”

Dylan Stigliano — SoFi

What’s New

Crypto-backed mortgages aren’t entirely new. Miami-based lender Milo has offered one since 2022, though it remains a niche product with just over 100 customers. [P2] What sets the Better Home product apart is its target audience — younger buyers with meaningful crypto holdings but limited cash savings — and its ambition to conform to Fannie Mae guidelines, which govern trillions in U.S. mortgages. Conforming status would unlock significantly lower interest rates than existing crypto-backed alternatives. [P3]

The catch: Fannie Mae hasn’t officially endorsed the product. It’s still unclear how the agency’s risk calculations would accommodate a volatile asset class like Bitcoin. [P4] That said, the regulatory backdrop is shifting. Last June, the Federal Housing Finance Agency directed both Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac to prepare to count cryptocurrency as a qualifying mortgage asset, signaling growing institutional openness under the current administration. [P5]

How It Works

At closing, borrowers take out two loans — a standard mortgage on the home and a separate loan secured by their crypto to cover the down payment. Better Home combines both into a single monthly payment at one interest rate, a structure that distinguishes it from prior arrangements. [P6] No additional collateral is required if the crypto drops in value, but significant overcollateralization is built in to cushion against volatility. For Bitcoin, that means pledging $250,000 to borrow $100,000.

The Risks

For buyers whose wealth is already heavily concentrated in crypto, using that same position to back a mortgage effectively doubles down on that exposure — a meaningful risk worth weighing carefully.

The Bigger Picture

Crypto-backed mortgages sit at a genuine crossroads. They’re credible enough to attract a major lender and the world’s leading crypto exchange. But regulatory acceptance remains uncertain, and near-term mainstream appeal is unclear. Still, the pattern is familiar: each time crypto finds a foothold in regulated finance — ETFs, retirement accounts, and now potentially mortgages — the question evolves. It shifts from whether digital assets belong in the financial system to how much risk that system is prepared to absorb. This product may be an early, telling test of that boundary.

Crypto Defined

Liquidity

Liquidity is how easily you can buy or sell an asset without moving its price. A liquid market has lots of buyers and sellers, tight spreads, and can absorb large trades without dramatic swings. An illiquid market means even a modest order can move the price significantly — which is why a single headline can move crypto prices 5 to 10% in minutes.

→ Why it matters this week: Any resolution — or escalation — in the Iran situation could move through crypto markets quickly. Crypto trades 24/7, meaning it prices in geopolitical shifts the moment they happen, before equity markets even open. [2]

→ Macro connection: When oil rises and the Fed holds rates, liquidity tightens across most risk assets. Less money moving through the system means prices become more sensitive to new information.

→ The limit: High liquidity can reduce volatility but not eliminate it. Even the most liquid markets in the world can move sharply on unexpected macro events. [A]

Number of the Day

$56.5B

Cumulative net inflows · U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs since launch

Since launch in January 2024, U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs have absorbed $56.5 billion in cumulative net inflows — with BlackRock’s IBIT holding the lion’s share at roughly $54 billion in assets under management. Even as Bitcoin fell more than 22% in Q1 2026, the funds pulled in $1.32 billion in March alone, ending a four-month outflow streak. That suggests institutions aren’t panic-selling; they’re buying the dip. And that structural demand marks a crucial difference between 2026 and the 2022 bear market. [5][A]
